## What You Are About to Do

You are going to build a web application.

That phrase might sound technical or intimidating, but here is what it means in plain language: you are going to create a set of files that, when published to the internet, allow real people to submit information, view community pages, and let an administrator review and manage what gets published.

This is the same kind of thing that websites like local news pages, nonprofit directories, and community bulletin boards do. You are going to build one from scratch, step by step, and by the end of it you will understand how it works — not just that it works.

---

## Why This Application Exists

The Community Ledger was built around a simple belief: communities deserve financial transparency, and the tools to achieve it should be accessible to anyone.

Many community organizations — mutual aid groups, neighborhood circles, local nonprofits, garden collectives — do meaningful work but have no public-facing way to show how they raise and spend money. This makes it harder to build trust, attract supporters, and hold themselves accountable.

This application gives communities a place to publish that information in a structured, honest, and readable way. Every community page that gets approved is a public act of transparency.

As you build your own version, you are not just learning to write code. You are learning to create infrastructure for trust.

---

## The Values Behind This Project

Before we write a single line of code, it is worth naming the values that shaped every decision in this application. These values are not decoration — they show up in technical choices.

**Transparency** — Financial information is visible to the public. The application is designed to make hiding easy to see and sharing easy to do.

**Accountability** — Submissions are reviewed before publication. Rejections come with reasons. Every approval and rejection is logged. The system assumes good faith and verifies it.

**Accessibility** — The tools used to build this application are all free. The skills required to maintain it can be learned by anyone. The communities it serves do not need to be wealthy or technically sophisticated to participate.

**Community ownership** — The application you build belongs to your community, not to a platform. You control the data. You control the rules. You control who administers it.

## What Artificial Intelligence Is — and What It Is Not

This tutorial was built with the help of an AI assistant. You may also choose to use one as you work through it. Before you do, it is important to understand exactly what that means.

### AI as a Tool for Human-Directed Tasks

Imagine a power drill. A power drill is a tool. It does not decide what to build. It does not know whether the wall you are drilling into is load-bearing or decorative. It does not care whether the shelf you are mounting will hold books or fall on someone.

You decide all of that. The drill just makes your hands faster.

AI, when used as a tool for human-directed tasks, works the same way. You decide what to build. You decide what values it should reflect. You decide when the result is good enough. The AI makes your hands faster — it can write boilerplate code, explain an error message, suggest a structure, or draft a document.

But it does not understand your community. It does not know your neighbors. It cannot decide what transparency means in your context. Only you can do that.

**This is the kind of AI use this tutorial models: you direct, AI assists.**

### AI for Personal Use vs. AI for Community Infrastructure

There is a meaningful difference between using AI to recommend a restaurant and using AI to build infrastructure that your community will depend on.

When you use AI to browse, recommend, or entertain, the stakes are low. If the AI is wrong about a movie, you watch a mediocre film.

When you use AI to build systems that collect data, make decisions about what gets published, or determine who has access to what — the stakes are different. Errors have consequences for real people. Biases in the AI's suggestions can become policies in your system. Shortcuts taken during development can become vulnerabilities that expose your community's data.

The difference is not the AI. The difference is the context and the responsibility.

**This tutorial asks you to take that responsibility seriously.** Every technical decision you make is also a values decision. AI can help you implement it. Only you can make it.

### What This Means Practically

- When an AI helps you write code in this tutorial, read it. Do not copy it blindly.
- When something works, ask yourself why it works.
- When something does not work, do not just ask AI to fix it — try to understand what broke.
- When you finish this tutorial, the application you have built should feel like yours, not like something you assembled from parts you do not understand.

## How This Tutorial Is Structured

Each section covers one concept or one step in the build. Sections are short enough to finish in a single sitting. At the end of each section there is a **Concept Check** — a set of open-ended questions.

These questions are not graded. There is no correct answer to memorize. They are invitations to think out loud — on paper, in a journal, in a conversation with someone else learning alongside you.

The research on learning is clear: people who explain things in their own words retain them. People who move on without reflecting often discover later that they only recognized the material, they did not actually understand it. Recognition and understanding feel the same in the moment. They are not the same.

Take the Concept Checks seriously.
